Elemental `Ma' The Space Between Nature's Forces

An architectural framework where water, air, earth, and fire are experienced through spatial intervals rather than symbolic forms.

Zoning is designed to present the spatial experience of the four

elements in a gradual and legible narrative. Public and shared use

areas are located in open and permeable zones that establish a

more direct relationship with wind and water, while more private

functions are resolved in sheltered areas related to topography

and mass continuity. The fire element reinforces the spatial

hierarchy by being located at gathering and pause points; thus,

zoning produces a spatial flow based on transitions between

elements

rather

than

functional

divisions.

Elemental Ma takes its name from the Japanese concept "ma," which describes the meaningful space, interval, and pause between elements. Elemental refers to the four fundamental forces of nature--water, air, earth, and fire. Together, the name defines an architectural approach in which space is shaped by the relationships between natural elements rather than by form alone.
Elemental Ma is conceived as a sequence of spatial intervals where natural forces are not represented but revealed. The project organizes water, air, earth, and fire as climatic and sensory experiences, allowing architecture to act as a mediator rather than a barrier. Through thresholds, pavilions, and voids, the building intensifies wind, collects rain, grounds the body, and introduces warmth, creating a continuous dialogue between human presence and
nature.

The structure naturally collects and directs rainwater thanks to its roof, which is shaped according to the prevailing wind direction. Being elevated above the ground, the structure follows the natural flow of water on the soil without interrupting it; it works with gravity and topography rather than imposing any coercive intervention. This approach embodies an architectural stance that harmonizes with rain rather than controlling it.
