Studio

A lab, not an office

We treat every brief as a research question — testing, prototyping and discarding until the building answers something the site has been asking all along.

THE O ARCHITECTURE LAB is an Istanbul-based practice working across architecture, urbanism and spatial research. The studio was founded on a simple conviction: design is strongest when it begins by listening — to people, to context, to material.

Our work spans private houses, public space, cultural buildings and interiors. Whatever the scale, the method stays the same: research first, form later. The "O" in our name is the circle we draw around a problem before we touch it.

What scale of project do you take on?

From single-room interiors to public buildings and urban studies. The method matters more to us than the square meterage — if the question is interesting, the size rarely disqualifies it.

Do you work outside Istanbul?

Yes. The studio is based in Istanbul but has delivered projects in Izmir and Ankara, and collaborates remotely with consultants across Europe. Site presence is arranged per project phase.

How are fees structured?

Typically as a percentage of construction cost for full-service design, or a fixed fee for research, feasibility and consultancy work. We scope this transparently in the first proposal — no hidden phases.

How long does a typical project take?

Concept design usually takes 6–10 weeks; full design development and documentation 4–8 months depending on scale. We would rather give you an honest timeline early than a flattering one we revise later.

What does the first meeting look like?

A conversation, not a pitch. Bring the site, the budget range and the doubts. We will tell you honestly whether the project needs us — and sometimes the honest answer is that it doesn't yet.

01 — Pavilion

Elemental Ma

The Space Between Nature's Forces

Overview

An architectural framework where water, air, earth, and fire are experienced through spatial intervals rather than symbolic forms.

Elemental Ma takes its name from the Japanese concept “ma,” which describes the meaningful space, interval, and pause between elements. Elemental refers to the four fundamental forces of nature — water, air, earth, and fire. Together, the name defines an architectural approach in which space is shaped by the relationships between natural elements rather than by form alone.

The project is conceived as a sequence of spatial intervals where natural forces are not represented but revealed. Through thresholds, pavilions, and voids, the building intensifies wind, collects rain, grounds the body, and introduces warmth — creating a continuous dialogue between human presence and nature.

The structure naturally collects and directs rainwater through a roof shaped according to the prevailing wind direction. Elevated above the ground, it follows the natural flow of water on the soil without interrupting it — working with gravity and topography rather than imposing any coercive intervention. An architectural stance that harmonizes with rain rather than controlling it.

The Four Elements

Water

Collection, sound, cooling

Air

Acceleration, permeability, breath

Earth

Mass, texture, grounding

Fire

Warmth, light, gathering

Zoning & Spatial Flow

Zoning presents the spatial experience of the four elements as a gradual, legible narrative. Public and shared areas sit in open, permeable zones that establish a direct relationship with wind and water, while private functions are resolved in sheltered areas tied to topography and mass continuity. The fire element reinforces the spatial hierarchy at gathering and pause points — producing a flow based on transitions between elements rather than functional divisions.

02 — Public Space

Common Ground

Izmir, Turkey — 2022

Overview

A reclaimed industrial plot in Alsancak converted into a shared civic space for a neighbourhood that had none. The brief asked for a plaza; the result is closer to a public room — enclosed enough to gather, open enough to pass through.

Seating, planting and a water element are embedded into a single cast-concrete topography rather than placed on top of a flat surface. The ground itself does the work.

Design Process

01 — Context Analysis

Mapping the neighbourhood's history, daily patterns, and gaps in civic infrastructure.

02 — Topographic Design

Creating a continuous ground surface that facilitates gathering and movement.

03 — Material Testing

Prototyping concrete finishes and plant selections for durability and beauty.

04 — Community Integration

Activation and programming — turning a space into a living civic room.

03 — Cultural

Archive of Light

Istanbul, Turkey — 2021

Overview

A photography archive and study centre housed in a former printing workshop in Karaköy. The original cast-iron structure is retained and expressed; new insertions are clearly legible as new. Light — the subject of the archive — becomes the primary design medium.

A system of sliding perforated screens controls the quality of light across reading rooms, print vaults and a public gallery throughout the day without mechanical assistance.

Design Process

01 — Heritage Study

Understanding the building's industrial past and structural logic.

02 — Light Studies

Mapping solar patterns and designing screen systems for optimal light control.

03 — Adaptive Insertion

Integrating new spaces within the existing structure without compromise.

04 — Activation

Creating spaces for research, exhibition, and public engagement.

04 — Interior

Soft Threshold

Ankara, Turkey — 2024

Overview

A 220 m² apartment interior in Çankaya where the client's brief was simply: make the rooms feel connected without removing the walls. The answer is a threshold system — deep doorways, level changes and material shifts that signal transition without hard division.

Warm plaster, unlacquered oak and hand-woven textiles provide continuity across all rooms while allowing each space its own intimate register.

Design Process

01 — Brief Understanding

Understanding the client's daily patterns and desire for spatial fluidity.

02 — Threshold Design

Developing a vocabulary of transitions — doorways, steps, material changes.

03 — Material Selection

Choosing materials that age beautifully — plaster, oak, textiles — for warmth.

04 — Custom Craft

Handmade textiles and bespoke joinery for the intimate scale of home.
